The mass has the axis of the cap for symmetry axis.  Put the origin at the
centre $O$ of the whole sphere and the $z$–axis along the symmetry
axis, the pole of the cap being at $z=R$.  The plane of the cut is the
plane

$$
z=a =R-h\qquad(0<h\le 2R)
$$

so that the cap is the solid

$$
a\le z\le R,\qquad 0\le r\le\sqrt{R^{2}-z^{2}}
$$

in the cylindrical coordinates $(r,\theta ,z)$.  For a homogeneous
body of (volume) density $\rho$ the $z$–coordinate of the centroid is

$$
\bar z=\frac{\displaystyle\int_{V}z\,dV}{\displaystyle\int_{V}dV},
\qquad dV=r\,dr\,d\theta \,dz,
$$

the density $\rho$ cancelling.  A slice $z=$ const. is a circular disc
of radius $\rho(z)=\sqrt{R^{2}-z^{2}}$ and area $\pi\rho^{2}=
\pi(R^{2}-z^{2})$; hence

$$
dV=\pi\,(R^{2}-z^{2})\,dz,\qquad a\le z\le R .
$$

The volume of the cap is

\begin{align*}
V&=\int_{a}^{R}\pi(R^{2}-z^{2})\,dz
   =\pi\Bigl[R^{2}z-\frac{z^{3}}{3}\Bigr]_{a}^{R} \\
  &=\frac{\pi}{3}\Bigl[\,2R^{3}-3R^{2}a+a^{3}\Bigr]
   =\frac{\pi}{3}(R-a)^{2}(2R+a).
\end{align*}

With $R-a=h$ and $2R+a=3R-h$,

$$
V=\int_{V}dV
   =\pi\int_{R-h}^{R}(R^{2}-z^{2})\,dz
   =\pi h^{2}\!\left(R-\frac{h}{3}\right)
   =\frac{\pi h^{2}}{3}\,(3R-h)\tag{1}
$$

(the ordinary formula for a spherical segment).  The first moment about the
plane $z=0$ is

\begin{align*}
M_{xy}&=\int_{V}z\,dV
      =\pi\int_{a}^{R}z\,(R^{2}-z^{2})\,dz
      =\pi\Bigl[\frac{R^{2}z^{2}}{2}-\frac{z^{4}}{4}\Bigr]_{a}^{R} \\
     &=\frac{\pi}{4}\Bigl(R^{2}-a^{2}\Bigr)^{2}
      =\frac{\pi}{4}\bigl[(R-a)(R+a)\bigr]^{2}.
\end{align*}

For $R-a=h$ and $R+a=2R-h$,

$$
M_{xy}= \pi\int_{R-h}^{R}z(R^{2}-z^{2})\,dz
     =\frac{\pi}{4}\,h^{2}(2R-h)^{2}\tag{2}
$$

directed along the axis toward the pole.  Because the body is uniform,
its centre of mass coincides with its centroid; from (1)–(2)

$$
\bar z=\frac{M_{xy}}{V}
      =\frac{\displaystyle \frac{\pi}{4}h^{2}(2R-h)^{2}}
             {\displaystyle\frac{\pi}{3}h^{2}(3R-h)}
      =\frac{3}{4}\,\frac{(2R-h)^{2}}{\,3R-h\,},\qquad a\le\bar z\le R,
$$

and $\bar x=\bar y=0$ by symmetry.  $\bar z$ is measured from the
centre $O$ of the original sphere toward the pole of the cap; hence the
mass-centre of the solid cap lies on the axis a distance

$$
\,z_{c}= \frac{3(2R-h)^{2}}{4(3R-h)}\,
$$

above (i.e. on the cap side of) the centre of the complete sphere.
The distance is reckoned positive toward the spherical surface; with
the usual sign convention $O$ is the origin and the flat face of the cap
is the plane $z=R-h$.

The distance measured from the flat face $z=R-h$ is

$$
d=\bar z-(R-h)=\frac{h(4R-h)}{4(3R-h)}\;,
$$

and from the vertex $z=R$ it is $R-\bar z=h(8R-3h)/[4(3R-h)]$.
In particular, for a hemisphere $h=R$,

$$
\bar z=\frac{3R}{8},\qquad d=\frac{3R}{8},
$$

the familiar centroid of a solid hemisphere $3R/8$ above its diametral
plane; for $h\to2R$ the cap becomes the whole sphere, $\bar z\to0$,
and for a very shallow cap $h\ll R$, $\bar z=R-2h/3+O(h^{2})$, i.e. the
centroid is a distance $2h/3$ below the pole and $h/3$ above the base,
as for a paraboloid.

If $\rho$ is the (constant) mass density, the
mass of the cap is $M=\rho V=\rho\pi h^{2}(3R-h)/3$, and its axial first
moment is $M\bar z=\rho M_{xy}$, so that $\bar z$ is indeed the
mass-weighted mean height.  The density $\rho$ cancels, so the result
is independent of the material and depends only on the geometry $R$ and
$h$. ∎
